Why healthcare technology projects fail after go-live.

Go-live is not the finish line. In healthcare, the real test starts when busy people must use the system under real operational pressure.

Main argument

Most failures are operating-model failures

Healthcare technology can be technically live while operationally dead. A system may be deployed, accessible and demonstrated, but still fail to change the work because the organization never redesigned ownership, workflow, training, incentives and measurement.

1. The workflow was never truly mapped

Teams often document the ideal process, not the real one. Real healthcare work includes exceptions, missing documents, urgent escalations, unclear responsibility, duplicate entry, informal communication and workarounds. If these are ignored, users will create their own parallel process.

2. Ownership is split across too many people

Digital-health work needs clear clinical, operational and technical ownership. If everyone is partially responsible, no one owns adoption, issue resolution or continuous improvement.

3. Training explains features, not behavior

Feature training is not enough. Users need to know when to use the system, what to stop doing, how to handle exceptions, where to escalate and how success will be measured.

4. Leadership measures the wrong finish line

Go-live, number of accounts and number of sessions are weak success measures by themselves. Better measures include turnaround time, completion rate, error reduction, user adoption, service quality, escalation volume, patient or employee experience and sustained usage.

5. There is no sustainment rhythm

After launch, the organization needs a rhythm for feedback, incidents, data quality, workflow improvement and benefits review. Without that rhythm, small issues quietly become permanent resistance.

What leaders should do differently

  • Require a real workflow map before approving build or purchase.
  • Name one accountable owner for adoption and value.
  • Create role-based SOPs, not generic manuals.
  • Measure operational outcomes, not only technical deployment.
  • Run post-launch improvement reviews for at least 90 days.

The best healthcare technology projects are not the ones that launch fastest. They are the ones that become trusted parts of daily work.
