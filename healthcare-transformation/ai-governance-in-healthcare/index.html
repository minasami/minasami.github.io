AI governance in healthcare

A practical framework for introducing artificial intelligence safely, responsibly and measurably across healthcare services and digital products.

Core idea: AI governance is the system of roles, decisions, controls and monitoring used to ensure that healthcare AI is safe, useful, accountable and appropriate for its intended context.

Why healthcare AI needs stronger governance

Healthcare AI can influence clinical decisions, patient communication, medication workflows, resource allocation and operational priorities. Governance should begin before model selection and continue throughout the full lifecycle.

The eight governance domains

  1. Intended use: define the problem, user, decision, setting and limits.
  2. Accountability: name the business, clinical, technical and risk owners.
  3. Data governance: control quality, provenance, privacy, consent and access.
  4. Validation: test safety, performance, bias, usability and workflow fit.
  5. Human oversight: define review, override and escalation responsibilities.
  6. Transparency: explain what the system does and where uncertainty exists.
  7. Monitoring: track drift, incidents, adoption and unintended effects.
  8. Lifecycle control: manage updates, versions, retirement and documentation.

A practical approval lifecycle

Screen the use case

Classify it by patient impact, autonomy, data sensitivity, scale and reversibility.

Define controls

Specify validation, human review, logging, escalation and rollback requirements.

Validate in context

Test the technology together with the real workflow and intended users.

Pilot safely

Use limited scope, measurable criteria, active monitoring and clear stop conditions.

Approve and deploy

Document responsibilities, limits, guidance and review dates.

Monitor and revalidate

Review performance after changes to data, workflow, population, policy or software.

Questions every AI project should answer

  • What decision or workflow is the AI supporting?
  • Who remains accountable for the final outcome?
  • Which populations may be underrepresented?
  • How will users recognize uncertainty or failure?
  • What events trigger escalation or suspension?
  • How will performance and value be measured?

Common governance failures

  • Buying a tool before defining the use case
  • Confusing technical accuracy with healthcare value
  • No named owner after deployment
  • Weak privacy or data provenance controls
  • No monitoring for drift or unintended consequences
  • No incident or rollback process
Useful principle: The greater the potential effect on patients, the stronger the need for human oversight, transparency, monitoring and accountability.
