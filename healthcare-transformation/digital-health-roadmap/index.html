Digital Health Strategy

How to build a digital health roadmap

A practical method for turning healthcare priorities into a sequenced portfolio of digital capabilities, platforms, workflows and adoption initiatives.

Core idea: A digital health roadmap is not a list of technologies. It is a sequence of business, clinical, operational, data and change capabilities tied to measurable outcomes.

Start with outcomes, not systems

Before choosing platforms, define what must improve. Examples include access, turnaround time, medication safety, continuity of care, patient engagement, staff workload, data visibility or service cost.

The six layers of a digital health roadmap

1. Outcomes

Patient, clinical, operational and financial goals.

2. Journeys

Patient, clinician, pharmacist and administrator workflows.

3. Capabilities

Scheduling, triage, records, analytics, communication and decision support.

4. Platforms

EHR, CRM, portals, integration, data and automation layers.

5. Foundations

Data quality, interoperability, privacy, cybersecurity and identity.

6. Adoption

Training, support, governance, incentives and behavior change.

A five-phase roadmap

Phase 1: Stabilize foundations

Clarify ownership, improve data quality, map workflows, strengthen privacy and resolve critical infrastructure gaps.

Phase 2: Digitize priority journeys

Focus on high-value workflows where digital tools can improve access, speed, safety or transparency.

Phase 3: Integrate and automate

Connect systems, reduce duplicate entry, automate routine handoffs and improve cross-team visibility.

Phase 4: Analyze and predict

Introduce dashboards, population insights, forecasting and decision support using trusted data.

Phase 5: Scale and optimize

Standardize successful models, expand adoption and continuously improve value realization.

How to prioritize initiatives

Score each initiative against expected value, strategic alignment, urgency, user demand, technical readiness, data readiness, dependency complexity, implementation risk and available capacity.

Useful rule: High-value, low-readiness initiatives should not be rejected. They should be preceded by foundation work that makes them feasible.

Roadmap governance

Assign clear owners for business outcomes, product decisions, architecture, data, privacy, change and benefits. Review the roadmap regularly because assumptions, technologies and organizational capacity change.

Common roadmap mistakes

  • Organizing the roadmap around vendors instead of outcomes
  • Ignoring workflow redesign
  • Launching too many initiatives at once
  • Underestimating integration and data quality
  • Treating training as a final-stage activity
  • Measuring deployment instead of adoption and value

What should the roadmap contain?

A useful roadmap shows outcome themes, capabilities, initiatives, dependencies, milestones, owners, risks, adoption activities and expected benefits. It should be understandable to executives without hiding technical dependencies from delivery teams.
